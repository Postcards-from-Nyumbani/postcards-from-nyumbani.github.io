There is a particular kind of teacher who does not know they are one.

I am thinking of the woman at the Kencom bus stage who, when a man shouted at her from a passing vehicle, did not look up from her phone. Not in defeat. In complete indifference. I watched her and thought: *I would like to learn that*.

I am thinking of my roommate in first year who kept a small notebook of things that made him laugh. Not a journal — he was very clear about this. A notebook, specifically, of things that were funny. He filled three of them in two years. I have thought about that practice almost every week since.

---

The lessons that arrive sideways are the ones that stay.

The ones delivered face-on — sit down, here is what you need to know — have a strange way of dissolving. I have forgotten more formal instruction than I can account for. But I remember the way a certain lecturer used to pause before answering a question, as if the question deserved the full weight of a real silence before he gave it a real answer. I try to do that now. I do not always succeed.

I remember a friend who, when she disagreed with you, would say *"I wonder if —"* and then make her point. Not *"I think"*, not *"actually"*. *"I wonder if."* It left space. You could still be right. She was almost always right, but she left space.

---

These people did not sign up to teach me anything. They were living their own lives, completely, and I happened to be nearby. That is the thing about paying attention: it is not polite. It is something closer to taking. You take what someone does not know they are giving.

I hope, somewhere, I am giving something I do not know I am giving.

I hope it is something worth keeping.
